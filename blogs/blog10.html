Things you need to know about coronavirus face masks

Masks are now your best accessory when it comes to battling COVID-19. Masks are all-important in stopping the spread of infection and preventing each individual from contracting airborne infections. Every cough, sneeze and conversation brings with it an opportunity to release germs into the air which may, in turn, pose a threat to everybody in the vicinity. Masks are an essential step in preventing cross-contamination in crowded spaces as an infection control strategy.

Facemasks come in different forms- each offering a different level of protection. Surgical grade N95 respirators offer the highest level of protection against COVID-19 infection, followed by surgical grade masks.

Respirators

Respirators are meant for frontline healthcare workers and are meant mainly to prevent exposure to tiny droplets that can remain suspended in the air. These masks are made of tangled fibers that help in filtering particles- certified N99 respirators can filter 99% of particles ranging from 100-300nm in size. N95 respirators filter out 95% while N100 respirators can filter up to 99.7% of these particles. These respirators must meet the rigorous filtration standards set by the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH).

Respirators can also have valves in-built into them which allow for exhaled air to leave the mask- allowing for easier breathing. However, this would mean that those around the person wearing the mask can fall prey to the particles and pathogens exhaled.

Routine testing of the respirators helps ensure proper size and fit, as well as testing for air leakages. These types of masks should be reserved for healthcare workers on the frontline and first responders.

Surgical masks

Disposable, single-use masks made from a rectangular, pleated cloth are most commonly known as surgical masks. These masks cover the mouth, nose and jawline and are made from a breathable synthetic fabric.

These masks are not subject to NIOSH filtration standards- they are not airtight around the area of they cover. Thus, their filtration standards vary from 10% to 90%. They reduce the risk of various respiratory illnesses.

In the current pandemic, wearing these surgical masks play a pivotal role in reducing the spread of the disease. They are fluid-resistant and provide some amount of protection against large respiratory droplets from exposure to sneezes and coughs. These, too, are used by healthcare professionals who need adequate supply.

Cloth masks

Cloth masks are less effective than their medically-certified counterparts. The masks are porous and ineffective against droplets expelled from sneezes and coughs since there is no tight seal around the mouth and nose. However, it is better to wear these masks than wear no mask at all. Cloth masks are available as multilayered (3-6 layers) cloth masks and cloth masks with a pouch to place a filter to increase the level of protection. These could be preferred over a single layer cloth mask.
