How N95 masks work

In order to be able to make the best choice when selecting a mask, one has to understand how the N95 masks work. The N95 mask is indeed available to the general public and effective against most airborne particles. Classified by the US National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health as a mask that can be used for air filtration, N95 masks are functionally equivalent to FFP2 respirators (European Union) and KN95 respirators (China).

The physics of how a mask works is not as simple as straightforward sieve-like filtration. Instead, differently sized particles are combated in different manners. The basic material structure, however, remains the same. Cloth that is sieve-like allows for particles to flow through without disruption unless the cloth itself is very fine. The problem with finely woven cloth is that it prevents the wearer of the mask from breathing effectively- causing further damage rather than alleviating the issue. Instead, masks are made from material with scattered fiber within its matrix in order to prevent particles from having a direct path to the wearers.

These scattered fibres act in different ways for differently sized particles. For larger particles, the mask simply blocks it by interception- simply put, large particles fail to enter the matrix of the fibre at all. Impaction is a term used for mid-size particles where in the dispersed fibres of the cloth cause a convoluted pathway for the particles and thus- driven by inertia of movement- the particles tend to get caught on the fibres when moving at high speeds down these paths. By the process of diffusion, the smallest of particles are caught by the fibres present in the cloth matrix. For sizes in-between the first two, the layering of cloth usually catches the vast majority of them. However, since these particles are too small for impaction and too large for diffusion, they are the prime agent in testing the effectiveness of masks.

Other than the cloth matrix, it is essential to make sure that the mask is indeed airtight while simultaneously allowing the wearer to breathe effectively. The nose and mouth are the main areas to avoid when preventing airborne infection. A tight seal, along with a non-sieve like fiber for the making of the mask itself, ensures the protection of the wearer.

As of now, N95 masks are mainly worn by healthcare professionals and front-line healthcare practitioners in the fight against COVID-19.
